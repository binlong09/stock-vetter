## Summary

Adobe is a Watchlist candidate with a compelling valuation anomaly at the center of the thesis. The reverse DCF implies the market is pricing in a -3.4% annual FCF contraction over the next decade, against a 3-year actual of 10% and 5-year actual of 13.2% — an extraordinarily wide gap that suggests either the market is deeply wrong or the AI disruption risk to Adobe's creative seat model is being priced as near-terminal. The business itself scores well on earnings quality and cyclicality resistance, underpinned by a subscription model with high cash conversion and predictable churn characteristics. The weakest dimension is capital allocation: buybacks have been executed at scale and at optically cheap prices, but the $11.28B repurchased in fiscal 2025 exceeded reported FCF, implying debt or cash drawdown rather than surplus capital — and no dividend or M&A pipeline provides a second leg to the capital return story. The core question this card cannot answer is whether the AI transition (seat shrinkage offset by generative credit pricing) will sustain or erode the revenue growth rate that the current valuation demands almost nothing of.

### What the disagreements mean

The most material disagreement between the analyst and primary-source data is on buyback quality. The analyst frames $9.5B in repurchases as countercyclical discipline; the 10-K data shows fiscal 2025 buybacks of $11.28B exceeded reported FCF entirely, implying the balance sheet was used as a funding source rather than surplus cash. When sources disagree on a fact — specifically, whether buybacks were within-FCF or not — trust the 10-K. A second disagreement on revenue growth is less resolvable: the analyst asserts continued high-single-digit growth without modeling it; primary sources note deceleration but neither source has current segment data. The valuation case is unusual in that it requires almost no growth to be justified by the reverse DCF, which partially defuses this disagreement — but it also means that if AI disruption does accelerate seat churn, the FCF could inflect negative faster than either source has modeled.

## Things to verify before acting

- Pull the fiscal 2025 10-K cash flow statement and reconcile: operating cash flow ($10.03B) vs. net buybacks ($11.28B) vs. debt issuance/repayment and ending cash balance. Confirm whether buybacks were funded by FCF surplus, debt drawdown, or existing cash — this directly determines whether capital allocation is disciplined or over-extended.
- On the next earnings call (Q1 FY2026), listen for any disclosure of generative credit attach rates or consumption-based revenue as a percentage of Creative Cloud ARR. This is the single most important leading indicator of whether the seat-shrinkage offset thesis is materializing.
- Calculate GAAP FCF (operating cash flow minus capex minus SBC) and compare to the analyst's ex-SBC '14x FCF' framing. If SBC runs at $1.5-2B annually, the GAAP FCF yield is meaningfully lower — verify that the valuation cheapness holds on the more conservative basis.
- Check the most recent Canva enterprise pricing page and any Affinity integration announcements. The Canva upmarket threat is the most concretely specifiable competitive risk; tracking whether Canva has crossed into $50k+ enterprise deal territory would update the moat durability score.
- Run the reverse DCF at a range of discount rates (8%, 10%, 12%) and terminal multiples (15x, 20x, 25x) to stress-test the -3.4% implied CAGR. At what assumed terminal multiple does Adobe require positive FCF growth to justify the current price? This tells you how much the terminal value assumption is doing in the bull case.
- Review the 'Remaining Performance Obligations' (RPO) disclosure in the most recent 10-Q. For a subscription business, RPO growth rate is a leading indicator of revenue trajectory 12-24 months forward and can confirm or refute the revenue deceleration concern before it shows up in reported revenue.
- Verify the $6.2B long-term debt maturity schedule in the 10-K notes. With net cash of ~$229M, confirm there are no near-term (1-3 year) maturities that would compete with buybacks for capital allocation priority and could force refinancing at higher rates.
