## Summary

Cadence is a Watchlist name, not a buy at current prices. The business is genuinely excellent — a duopoly-like position in EDA software anchored by switching-cost moats in Virtuoso and Palladium, with ~80% recurring revenue smoothing the underlying cyclicality of its semiconductor customers. The problem is valuation: the reverse DCF implies 19.6% annual FCF growth over ten years to justify today's price, which is well above the recent 3-year actual of 12.4% and demanding even relative to the impressive 5-year track record of 30.9% (likely inflated by COVID-era semiconductor capex). Trading at 84.7x earnings and 68.1x EV/EBIT — both at meaningful premiums to already-elevated 10-year medians — the stock offers almost no margin of safety, and the analyst video makes this point more sharply: at a price 17% above the video's reference, even the bear case implies deeply negative annualized returns.

### What the disagreements mean

When a source omits a disclosed risk that appears in the 10-K, trust the 10-K. The two sources do agree on the bottom line — the stock is too expensive — but the primary-source score is more forgiving (Watchlist vs. Pass) because it weighs the business quality dimensions independently of price; the analyst's Pass verdict is essentially a pure valuation call that, if anything, has strengthened since the video was recorded.

## Things to verify before acting

- Pull the most recent 10-K or 10-Q and calculate SBC as a percentage of revenue. If it is running above 8%, restate owner earnings by subtracting SBC before applying the reverse DCF — this alone could add 2–3 percentage points to the required implied growth rate, making the valuation case even harder to justify.
- Check Cadence's China revenue as a disclosed percentage in the geographic segment footnotes (typically in the revenue recognition note). If China is still 15–18% of revenue, model a scenario where that segment declines 30–50% due to export control escalation and estimate the resulting FCF haircut.
- Compare the 2025 FCF/net income ratio of 1.43x to the prior three years to determine whether the divergence is structural (e.g., deferred revenue growth outpacing bookings) or a one-time working capital benefit. A ratio that reverts toward 1.0x would reduce the FCF base used in the reverse DCF.
- Listen to the most recent earnings call transcript (available on Cadence's investor relations page) and note management's commentary on AI-driven EDA demand. Specifically look for whether they quantify AI-design pipeline growth or whether the narrative is qualitative — a key uncertainty given how much of the growth story depends on AI tailwinds.
- Track the Synopsys–Ansys merger integration progress. Synopsys now has a multi-physics simulation capability that Cadence lacks; if Synopsys begins bundling Ansys tools into EDA deals, check Cadence's gross retention rates in the next two annual reports for any degradation in the 90%+ range currently implied.
- Run a simple downside scenario: assume FCF CAGR of 10% for 10 years (the lower bound of recent history) and an exit multiple of 22x FCF (a compression from current levels). Calculate the implied price and compare to today's entry — this is the bear-case return the analyst estimated as deeply negative, and you should confirm whether your own cost of capital assumption changes the conclusion.
